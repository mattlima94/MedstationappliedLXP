2. Unit Economics for Healthcare

Unit economics help healthcare organizations understand the profitability of each patient relationship. These metrics guide decisions about marketing, operations, and growth strategy.

üìä Healthy Benchmarks
  • LTV:CAC Ratio: 3:1 or higher (every $1 spent on acquisition should return $3+)
  • CAC Payback: <12 months (recover acquisition cost within first year)
  • Churn Rate: <2-3% monthly for primary care (patients stay 3-5+ years)

Why Unit Economics Matter

Understanding unit economics helps answer critical business questions:

  • How much can we spend on marketing? If LTV is $2,000 and you want 3:1 LTV:CAC, max CAC is ~$660
  • Should we offer discounts? Depends on whether reduced revenue is offset by longer tenure
  • Where should we open new clinics? Markets with higher LTV justify higher CAC
  • Is our retention strategy working? Track churn rate and LTV over time

3. Payer Dynamics & Reimbursement

Healthcare providers are paid through multiple channels, each with different rules, rates, and administrative requirements. Understanding payer dynamics is essential for financial management.

Payer Types

Payer Type Coverage Typical Reimbursement
Commercial Insurance Employer-sponsored, individual plans Highest rates (negotiated)
Medicare 65+, disabled, ESRD Moderate (fee schedule)
Medicaid Low-income individuals and families Lowest rates
Self-Pay Uninsured or elective services Variable (often discounted)

Reimbursement Models

  • Fee-for-Service (FFS): Payment per service rendered. Volume-driven. Traditional model.
  • Value-Based Care (VBC): Payment tied to quality outcomes and cost efficiency. Growing rapidly.
  • Capitation: Fixed monthly payment per patient regardless of services. Risk-bearing.
  • Bundled Payments: Single payment for episode of care (e.g., joint replacement).
üí° Key Concept

Payer Mix: The proportion of revenue from each payer type significantly impacts profitability. A practice with 60% commercial insurance will have higher margins than one with 60% Medicaid, even with identical patient volumes.

4. Financial Drivers in Primary Care

Understanding what drives financial performance helps operations professionals make better decisions.

Revenue Drivers

  • Patient Volume: More visits = more revenue (FFS model)
  • Service Mix: Higher-reimbursing services improve margins
  • Payer Mix: Commercial > Medicare > Medicaid
  • Collection Rate: Percentage of charges actually collected
  • Coding Accuracy: Proper coding captures appropriate reimbursement

Cost Drivers

  • Labor: 50-60% of primary care costs (providers, staff, benefits)
  • Occupancy: Rent, utilities, maintenance
  • Technology: EHR, practice management, telehealth platforms
  • Supplies: Medical supplies, office supplies
  • Administrative: Billing, compliance, insurance
