1. What is HIPAA?

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) was enacted in 1996 to protect sensitive patient health information from being disclosed without the patient's consent or knowledge.

HIPAA's Three Main Rules

Rule Purpose Key Requirements
Privacy Rule Protects PHI in any form Limits use/disclosure, patient rights, minimum necessary standard
Security Rule Protects electronic PHI (ePHI) Administrative, physical, and technical safeguards
Breach Notification Rule Requires disclosure of breaches Notify patients, HHS, and media (if large breach)
üí° Key Concept

Who Must Comply? HIPAA applies to "covered entities" (healthcare providers, health plans, healthcare clearinghouses) and their "business associates" (vendors who handle PHI). Medstation is a covered entity, and all team members must comply.

3. Permitted Uses & Disclosures

HIPAA doesn't prohibit all sharing of PHI‚Äîit controls how PHI can be used and disclosed. Understanding permitted uses is essential.

PHI May Be Used/Disclosed For:

Purpose Example Authorization Required?
Treatment Sharing records with specialist for referral No
Payment Submitting claim to insurance company No
Healthcare Operations Quality improvement, training, audits No
With Patient Authorization Releasing records to employer, attorney Yes
Public Health Activities Reporting communicable diseases No
Legal Requirements Court orders, subpoenas No (if properly ordered)
üí° Key Concept: Minimum Necessary Standard

When using or disclosing PHI, you must limit the information to the minimum necessary to accomplish the purpose. Don't share an entire medical record when only the diagnosis is needed for billing.

4. Common HIPAA Violations

HIPAA violations can result in serious consequences‚Äîfines ranging from $100 to $1.5 million per violation category per year, plus potential criminal charges. Learn to recognize and avoid these common violations.

üö´ Common Violations to Avoid
  • Discussing patients in public areas: Elevators, cafeterias, hallways where others can hear
  • Leaving PHI visible: Computer screens, papers on desks, faxes in open trays
  • Unauthorized access: Looking up records of friends, family, celebrities, or coworkers
  • Improper disposal: Throwing PHI in regular trash instead of shredding
  • Sharing login credentials: Letting others use your EHR access
  • Texting PHI: Using unsecured personal phones to share patient information
  • Social media posts: Any reference to specific patients, even without names
  • Lost/stolen devices: Unencrypted laptops, phones, USB drives with PHI

HIPAA Penalty Tiers

Tier Violation Type Penalty Range
Tier 1 Unknowing (didn't know) $100 - $50,000 per violation
Tier 2 Reasonable cause (should have known) $1,000 - $50,000 per violation
Tier 3 Willful neglect, corrected $10,000 - $50,000 per violation
Tier 4 Willful neglect, not corrected $50,000+ per violation
‚ö†Ô∏è Criminal Penalties

Knowingly obtaining or disclosing PHI can result in criminal charges: up to 1 year in prison for basic violations, up to 5 years for false pretenses, and up to 10 years for commercial advantage or malicious harm.

6. What To Do If You Suspect a Breach

üìã Breach Response Steps
  1. Stop the breach if possible (log out, secure documents)
  2. Report immediately to your supervisor or Privacy Officer
  3. Document what happened, when, and what PHI was involved
  4. Don't discuss the breach with anyone except those investigating
  5. Cooperate fully with the investigation

Early reporting minimizes harm and demonstrates good faith. Never try to hide or cover up a potential breach.

7. Key Takeaways

üìã Summary
  • HIPAA protects PHI through Privacy, Security, and Breach Notification Rules.
  • PHI = health info + any of 18 identifiers (name, DOB, SSN, MRN, etc.).
  • TPO (Treatment, Payment, Operations) doesn't require patient authorization.
  • Minimum necessary: Share only what's needed for the purpose.
  • Use only approved channels for PHI (EHR, secure portal, encrypted systems).
  • Report breaches immediately‚Äîearly reporting reduces penalties.
  • Violations have serious consequences‚Äîfines up to $1.5M+ and criminal charges.
