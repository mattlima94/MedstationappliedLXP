2. Stakeholder Interviewing

Effective interviews uncover the real problems behind stated problems. This skill is essential for projects like patient retention analysis or process improvement.

Interview Framework: SPIN

Type Purpose Example Questions
Situation Understand current state "Walk me through how scheduling works today."
Problem Identify pain points "What's the most frustrating part of this process?"
Implication Understand impact "How does this affect patient satisfaction?"
Need-Payoff Explore solutions "If we solved this, what would change for you?"

‚úì DO

  • Prepare questions in advance
  • Listen more than you talk (80/20 rule)
  • Ask follow-up "why" questions
  • Take detailed notes
  • Summarize back what you heard
  • Thank them for their time

‚úó DON'T

  • Lead the witness with assumptions
  • Interrupt or finish sentences
  • Jump to solutions too early
  • Dismiss concerns as unimportant
  • Record without permission
  • Share what others told you
üéØ Pro Tip

End every interview with: "Is there anything else I should have asked you?" and "Who else should I talk to about this?" These questions often uncover the most valuable insights.

3. Professional Documentation

Clear documentation ensures your work is understood, actionable, and preserves institutional knowledge. Every fellowship deliverable requires strong documentation.

Documentation Principles

  • Audience-first: Write for the reader (CEO needs different detail than developer)
  • Executive summary: Lead with conclusions and recommendations
  • Structure: Use headings, bullets, and white space
  • Evidence: Support claims with data and quotes
  • Action-oriented: End with clear next steps

Document Structure Template

üìÑ Standard Document Structure
  1. Executive Summary (1 paragraph: problem, findings, recommendation)
  2. Background (Context the reader needs)
  3. Methodology (How you gathered information)
  4. Findings (What you learned, with evidence)
  5. Analysis (What the findings mean)
  6. Recommendations (Specific actions to take)
  7. Next Steps (Who does what by when)
  8. Appendix (Supporting details, data, interview notes)

4. Presenting to Leadership

Your final fellowship presentation to the CEO is a key evaluation moment. Effective presentations are structured, confident, and action-oriented.

Presentation Structure (15 minutes)

Section Time Content
Hook 1 min Grab attention with key finding or question
Context 2 min Problem statement, why it matters
Approach 2 min How you investigated (methodology)
Findings 4 min Key discoveries, supported by evidence
Recommendations 3 min Specific actions, expected impact
Ask 1 min What you need from leadership
Q&A 5 min Answer questions confidently

‚úì Presentation DO's

  • Practice out loud (minimum 3 times)
  • Make eye contact with audience
  • Speak slowly and clearly
  • Use visuals to support points
  • Anticipate questions
  • "I don't know, but I'll find out" is okay

‚úó Presentation DON'Ts

  • Read slides word-for-word
  • Cram too much on each slide
  • Apologize for your work
  • Go over time
  • Get defensive during Q&A
  • Make up answers you don't know

5. Giving & Receiving Feedback

Feedback is how we grow. During the fellowship, you'll receive daily feedback from mentors and give feedback to teammates. Both require skill.

Receiving Feedback

üí° The Right Mindset

Feedback is a gift. Even poorly delivered feedback usually contains something useful. Your job is to extract the signal from the noise, not to defend yourself.

  • Listen fully before responding
  • Ask clarifying questions: "Can you give me an example?"
  • Assume positive intent: They want you to succeed
  • Thank them: "I appreciate you telling me this"
  • Reflect: What can I learn from this?
  • Act: Make visible changes based on feedback

Giving Feedback (SBI Model)

Element Description Example
Situation When and where "In yesterday's team meeting..."
Behavior What you observed (specific, objective) "...you interrupted Maria twice while she was explaining her analysis..."
Impact Effect on you/team/work "...which made it hard for us to understand her point and seemed to discourage her."
‚ö†Ô∏è Important

Focus on behavior, not character. "You interrupted" (behavior) is actionable. "You're rude" (character) is not. Good feedback is specific, timely, and focused on improvement.
